Booking.com for Business

2021-2025

Role

Lead Product Designer

Stakes

650,000 existing customers, technology migration, and a rapid go-live causing friction for SME users.

Bet

Rapidly diagnose trust and usability issues, then ship targeted fixes to reverse churn and lift activation.

Outcome

  • Acquisition +20%
  • Mobile acquisition +160%
  • Activation +9%

The journey to reverse the churn

1) Using a lean, heuristic review of the new customer experience to achieve leadership alignment

Problem: The business recognised issues with migrating B4B to our own tech and UX, but knowledge was fragmented and people focused on implementation without a holistic view. We needed clarity of where the first‑time experience failed to guide roadmap improvements, however time was a critical factor.

Action:

  • Ran a heuristic evaluation of the end‑to‑end journey: sign‑up → dashboard → hotel search/list/detail → checkout.
  • Severity‑rated issues and bucketed by funnel stage (sign‑up, setup, itinerary, extras, confirm) so we could prioritise where the biggest pains were likely to be felt.
  • Highlighted SME realities (casual policies, consumer expectations, low allegiance) and trust breakers (confusing entry, inconsistent UI feedback).
  • Simulated a first‑time booking and played this back to the leadership team to help them understand what people were experiencing as new customers. This had the greatest impact, demonstrating how many, small UX issues combined to create a poor overall experience.

2) Stopping early funnel bleed by putting search on the dashboard

Problem: With a large existing user base, our priority needed to focus not on sign-up fixes, but immediate conversion. The biggest hurdle? A missing dashboard search left users stranded.

Action: I led the design of an MVP search form under significant engineering constraints to get functional utility out quickly. It wasn't pretty or ideal, but it was a high value-to-effort improvement for conversion.

Outcome: Early exits decreased, time‑to‑first‑search improved and activation lifted.

3) A fast-follow to the search form to improve activation further

Problem: An initial trade-off with the dashboard search meant we required a named traveller up-front. I had earlier identified this as a significant likely friction point and data proved this to be a major contributor to continued drop-off.

Action: I led a redesigned entry to use generic traveller counts (e.g., “2 travellers”) and cascaded the model through checkout for coherence.

Outcome: Activation lifted. However, trust remained a key gap.

4) Rebuilding for trust by realigning the hotel shopping funnel UX with Booking.com

Problem: My initial review, plus additional data points from CSAT and testing indicated significant trust concerns remained. Users questioned legitimacy, since the design language and execution were not aligned with Booking.com. This would need to be resolved before adding more features made sense.

Action: I led the realignment of interaction and visual patterns to better match the Booking.com leisure site. I also worked closely with the newly formed design system team to prove out a new design system, with hotel shopping as the first use case.

Outcome: Activation increased and trust improved, but we still needed stronger pre‑booking confidence. The design system proved its value by enabling fast execution and better standardisation across design and build, unlocking critical A/B testing capability.

5) Giving people early confidence to join and start a booking by rebuilding the sign‑up flow and dashboard

Problem: While the booking funnel was the primary focus initially, users still have to traverse through sign-up and dashboard to get there. All these steps were still un-optimised, contributing heavily to remaining acquisition and conversion concerns.

Action:

  • I led a redesign of the sign-up flow and dashboard, emphasising Booking.com brand alignment, customer value, personalisation and mobile optimisation.
  • Ran several workshops with wider group, generating 100+ ideas.
  • The ideas were distilled into four directions which were prototyped and tested, giving us a clearer signal on which might work best.

Outcome:

  • User testing indicated a short sign-up with built-in smarts and simple personalisation was the clear winner. Adding extra steps was seen as potentially useful but ultimately likely to cause drop-offs, as people just want to get in to 'self-test' the product as soon as possible.
  • Personalisation was a triple-win, as users were able to better understand the intention and benefits of the platform before finishing sign-up, and feel like it was being tailored to them. The result was early stickiness, and from a business perspective we were able to get better data on what peoples needs were during signup.
  • Mobile acquisition jumped +160%, matching desktop and driving the majority of the overall acquisition uplift.
  • Activation into making a booking increased by +9%.
  • A simple copy change I championed, from “Which company would you like to Register?” to “Which company do you work for?” increased registration by +11.5% in A/B testing.
  • Pre‑populating the company logo when a corporate email is detected showed a +3.5% increase in registration with A/B testing, for these users.
